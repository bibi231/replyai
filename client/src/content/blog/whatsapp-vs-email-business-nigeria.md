Nigeria is a WhatsApp-first country for business communication. But using WhatsApp for everything creates problems that email was designed to solve. Here's how to use each channel correctly.

## Why WhatsApp dominates Nigerian business

WhatsApp has 3 advantages in the Nigerian context:
1. **Ubiquity** — virtually every Nigerian professional uses it, regardless of industry
2. **Speed** — voice notes, quick confirmations, instant file sharing
3. **Relationship warmth** — a personal number feels more accessible than a corporate email

For quick coordination, informal updates, and relationship building, WhatsApp is often the better choice.

## The WhatsApp trap

The problem: WhatsApp is terrible for:
- **Record-keeping** — messages get buried, chat backups are unreliable
- **Searchability** — finding a specific document sent 6 months ago is painful
- **Professional escalation** — a dispute that needs documentation shouldn't live in WhatsApp
- **Large attachments** — WhatsApp compresses files, degrading quality
- **Multi-party coordination** — more than 3 people in a chat becomes noise

Many Nigerian freelancers and small businesses run their entire client relationship through WhatsApp and then can't find the approval for scope change, the agreed price, or the final delivery confirmation.

## The channel decision framework

**Use WhatsApp when:**
- Quick yes/no decisions needed in real time
- Relationship-building casual check-ins
- Sharing location / meeting coordination
- Voice notes explaining a complex situation quickly
- Your client prefers it and the stakes are low

**Use email when:**
- Formal proposals, contracts, invoices
- Project deliverables
- Complaints or disputes
- Any communication you may need to reference later
- Multiple stakeholders need to be included
- Attaching documents at full quality
- International clients who don't use WhatsApp

**Use both (email first, WhatsApp to confirm):**
"Hi [Name], I just sent you the contract draft — let me know if you received it and have any questions."

This hybrid approach works well: email creates the record, WhatsApp creates the touchpoint.

## WhatsApp Business features worth using

If you're using WhatsApp for business, use **WhatsApp Business** (not the regular app):
- Quick replies (pre-saved responses to common questions)
- Labels (organize chats by stage: prospect, active client, invoice sent)
- Business profile (hours, website, email)
- Away message (auto-reply when you're unavailable)
- Catalog (for product-based businesses)

## The email → WhatsApp escalation rule

Some conversations start in email and need to move to WhatsApp for speed. The move works well when:
- A deadline is approaching and you need quick confirmation
- The email thread has become too long and a voice note would be faster
- Both parties prefer the channel

The move fails when:
- You're trying to avoid creating a paper trail (this is how disputes happen)
- The other party doesn't want to mix personal and professional channels

## Handling clients who only use WhatsApp

Some Nigerian clients — particularly in retail, hospitality, or informal sectors — will not use email. They respond to WhatsApp and ignore email. If this is your client, adapt:

1. Always request WhatsApp confirmation of key agreements: "Just confirming via WhatsApp: we agreed to ₦250,000 for the logo + brand guide, delivered in 2 weeks. Reply '✓' to confirm."
2. Screenshot important approvals and save them to Google Drive / email to yourself
3. Formally send summary emails anyway — even if they don't reply, you have the record
