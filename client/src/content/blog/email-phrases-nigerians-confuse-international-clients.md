Nigerian business English is rich, formal, and expressive. But some phrases that are perfectly natural in Nigerian professional communication create confusion or unintended impressions for international clients. Here are the most common ones.

## 1. "I am in receipt of your email"

**What it means in Nigeria:** "I received your email" — a formal acknowledgment.

**How it reads internationally:** Archaic. Most UK and US professionals haven't used "in receipt of" in 20 years. It signals very formal British English from an earlier era, which can feel distant or bureaucratic.

**Better alternative:** "Thank you for your email" or simply start with your response.

---

## 2. "Please do the needful"

**What it means in Nigeria:** "Please take care of this / please do what needs to be done."

**How it reads internationally:** Confusing. The phrase is almost exclusively used in South Asian and West African English. A British or American client will genuinely not know what action is being requested.

**Better alternative:** Be specific. "Please process the payment by Friday" or "Please confirm your availability for the meeting."

---

## 3. "I am humbly requesting..."

**What it means in Nigeria:** A respectful, deferential way to make a request. Shows appropriate respect to a superior or valued client.

**How it reads internationally:** Overly self-deprecating. In Western business communication norms, "humbly" signals low confidence or excessive deference that can undermine your professional positioning.

**Better alternative:** "I'd like to request..." or simply make the request directly: "Could you please review the attached proposal?"

---

## 4. "With due respect..." or "No offense, but..."

**What it means in Nigeria:** A pre-emptive courtesy before saying something that might push back on the other person's view.

**How it reads internationally:** A signal that something uncomfortable is coming — which it is. But the phrase is so associated with passive-aggression in Western communication that it primes the reader to be defensive before they've read your actual point.

**Better alternative:** Just say the thing. If you disagree, state your perspective directly: "I see it differently — [your view]." This is more professional and more effective.

---

## 5. "At the earliest convenience" and "at your earliest" combined with "please"

**Example:** "Please do this at your earliest convenience please"

**What it means in Nigeria:** A polite way of saying this is time-sensitive.

**How it reads internationally:** The doubled "please" reads as either a formatting error or unusual formality. "At your earliest convenience" is fine but can actually signal the opposite of urgency — it sounds optional.

**Better alternative:** State the actual urgency: "I need this by [specific date/time]" or "When you have a moment today" if it's genuinely flexible.

---

## Why these patterns exist

These phrases aren't errors — they reflect a formal British English tradition that's been maintained and enriched in Nigerian professional culture. Many of them are grammatically correct and were standard business English 50 years ago.

The issue is register: international clients in the US, Canada, and Western Europe now use a more casual, direct professional register. The formality gap can create a perception of stiffness or occasionally, urgency signals that get lost.

## What to keep

Not all distinctly Nigerian professional phrases should be dropped. Many Nigerian business communication norms are superior to Western alternatives:

- More thorough context-setting before requests (helps the reader understand why something is important)
- Explicit politeness markers (Nigerian emails are rarely read as cold or curt)
- Stronger relationship-building in professional correspondence

Adapt the phrases that cause confusion. Keep the relationship-building warmth that Nigerian professional English does better than most.
