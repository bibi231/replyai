Most professionals spend 2–4 hours per day on email. A significant portion of that time is staring at an email, knowing what they want to say, but unsure how to say it well. This guide gives you a repeatable framework.

## The 4-part email reply structure

Every professional email reply has four components:

**1. Acknowledge** — confirm you received and understood the email
**2. Answer** — respond directly to what was asked
**3. Act** — state what you're doing or what happens next
**4. Ask** — if needed, ask one clear question (never more than one)

Example:

> "Thank you for sending the contract draft. I've reviewed it and have two questions before I can sign:
>
> 1. Section 4.2 references a payment schedule that isn't attached — could you send that?
> 2. The termination clause mentions 30 days but our verbal agreement was 14 — can you confirm which applies?
>
> Once I have those clarifications, I can return the signed contract within 24 hours."

This reply: acknowledges receipt, gives the specific feedback, states a clear timeline, asks exactly the questions needed. It took 3 minutes to write and moves the deal forward.

## Tone calibration

The tone of your reply signals your relationship with the sender. Get it wrong and you either seem cold or too casual.

**Professional (default for external contacts, senior people, new relationships):**
- Complete sentences
- No slang or contractions
- Specific, factual language

**Friendly (established relationships, peers, startup context):**
- Contractions acceptable ("I'll", "we've")
- Some warmth in opening/closing
- Can acknowledge personal elements ("Hope the Lagos traffic wasn't too bad today!")

**Firm (when setting limits, declining requests, or addressing problems):**
- Direct and factual
- No excessive apologizing
- Clear statement of what will and won't happen

**Apologetic (when you've made a mistake or are delivering bad news):**
- Lead with the apology (don't bury it)
- Explain what happened without over-explaining or making excuses
- State clearly what you're doing to fix it

## The most common reply mistakes

### 1. Burying the answer

Starting with 3 paragraphs of context before answering the question makes the reader work harder than they should.

Bad: "Thanks for your email. I hope you're well. As we discussed last week, our team has been reviewing the proposal. We've had several internal meetings about it. After careful consideration and consultation with our stakeholders..."

Good: "Yes, we'd like to proceed. I'll have the contract to you by Friday."

### 2. Answering a different question

Re-read the email before replying. It sounds obvious but many people skim incoming emails and answer what they expect was asked.

### 3. Too many questions in one reply

If you need 5 pieces of information, ask for the most blocking one first. Getting a 5-question reply is discouraging for the sender and delays the chain.

### 4. Passive-aggressive tone

"As per my last email..." is noticed. "As I previously stated..." is noticed. If someone missed your earlier email, just resend it with context.

### 5. Not setting a clear next step

Emails that end with "Let me know what you think" start a new cycle of uncertainty. End with:
- A specific question: "Can you confirm by Thursday?"
- A specific action: "I'll send the revised proposal by 3pm today."
- A clear decision: "If I don't hear from you by Friday, I'll proceed with Option A."

## Templates for common scenarios

### Following up on a proposal:
"Hi [Name], Following up on the proposal I sent on [date]. I'd love to hear your thoughts — is there anything I can clarify or adjust? If you'd like to talk through it, I'm free [available times]."

### Declining a request politely:
"Thank you for thinking of me for [request]. Unfortunately I'm not able to take this on right now because [brief reason]. I'd recommend [alternative/referral] who may be better placed to help."

### Acknowledging a delay:
"I apologize for the delayed response — [brief explanation]. To answer your question: [direct answer]. Going forward, [how you'll prevent recurrence]."
