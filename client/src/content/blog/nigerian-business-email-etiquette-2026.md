Email etiquette in Nigeria blends formal British-style business writing with distinct local conventions. Getting it wrong can cost you deals; getting it right builds trust faster than you expect.

## Greetings and salutations

**The formal standard:**
- "Dear Mr./Mrs./Dr. [Surname]" — always use title + surname for first contact
- "Good morning/afternoon/evening, [First name]" — once you've established rapport
- "Hi [First name]" — acceptable in startup/tech environments or after multiple exchanges

**What to avoid:**
- "Hey" — too casual for any Nigerian business context
- "To whom it may concern" — signals you didn't research who to contact
- Starting with no greeting at all — feels rude in the Nigerian context

**Honorifics matter.** If someone has a PhD, Chief title, or Alhaji designation, use it. Omitting it when it's known is noticed.

## Opening lines

Don't start with "I hope this email finds you well." It's overused and signals templated, impersonal communication.

Better alternatives:
- "Following our conversation at [event], I wanted to..." (specific, shows you remember)
- "Congratulations on [recent achievement] — I've been following [company/project]..." (genuine, not flattering)
- "Per your request at yesterday's meeting..." (direct, professional)

If you genuinely don't know the person, be direct: "I'm [Name], [Role] at [Company]. I'm reaching out because..."

## Body structure

Nigerian business emails tend to be more contextual than Western emails — providing background before getting to the request is expected, not verbose.

**For a request email:**
1. Context (2–3 sentences: who you are, why you're writing)
2. The ask (1–2 sentences: what you need, by when)
3. What you're offering in return (if applicable)
4. Next step (clear call to action)

**For a response email:**
1. Acknowledge what was asked ("Thank you for your email regarding...")
2. Answer directly
3. Offer follow-up if needed

## Timing and follow-up

Business hours in Nigeria: 8am–5pm WAT (GMT+1). Sending at 7am will be seen as the first email of their morning. Sending at 10pm may feel intrusive.

**Follow-up timeline:**
- First follow-up: 3 business days after no response
- Second follow-up: 5 business days after first follow-up
- Third follow-up: 7–10 business days, then let it go

**Follow-up tone progression:**
1. First: Neutral reminder ("Just following up on my email below...")
2. Second: Slightly more direct ("I wanted to make sure this didn't get lost...")
3. Third: Clear signal ("I'll take it that this isn't a priority right now — please let me know if circumstances change")

## Closings

**Formal:**
- "Yours faithfully" — for "Dear Sir/Madam" (when you don't know the name)
- "Yours sincerely" — for named recipients in formal contexts
- "Best regards" / "Kind regards" — standard professional

**Semi-formal:**
- "Best" — widely acceptable
- "Warm regards" — warm but professional

**Avoid:**
- "Cheers" — very British, sounds affected in Nigerian context
- "Blessings" / "God bless" — increasingly common but not universally appropriate for first contact

## Language considerations

Nigerian business emails sometimes mix Pidgin phrases with formal English, especially internally or in startup contexts. For external professional communications, stick to standard English.

Exceptions:
- WhatsApp messages (yes, it's often the backup channel)
- Follow-up messages after rapport is established
- Startup/tech industries where cultural code-switching is common
