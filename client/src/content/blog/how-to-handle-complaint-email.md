Complaint emails are uncomfortable. They're also opportunities. How you handle them determines whether you lose a customer permanently or earn their long-term loyalty.

## The psychology of complaint emails

When someone writes a complaint email, they typically want three things:
1. To feel heard
2. To understand what happened
3. To know it won't happen again (or to be compensated for the impact)

What they don't want:
- To be blamed, even subtly
- Corporate-speak that doesn't acknowledge the real problem
- A reply that took 4 days to arrive

## The HEARD framework

**H** — Hear them (acknowledge specifically what they said, not a generic "I understand your frustration")
**E** — Empathize (make it clear you understand the impact, not just the event)
**A** — Acknowledge responsibility (if you're responsible, say so directly)
**R** — Respond (tell them what you're doing about it)
**D** — Do it (follow through on whatever you said)

## Template: Service delivery failure

Subject: Re: [Their subject]

Dear [Name],

Thank you for taking the time to write to us about this. I've read your email carefully and I want to address it directly.

[Specific acknowledgment of what went wrong — their specific complaint, not a generic version]

I'm sorry this happened. [Specific empathy for the impact — not "I understand your frustration" but "I understand this delayed your project deadline" or "I understand this was embarrassing in front of your client"]

[What happened / why — brief, no over-explaining, only if it's genuinely useful context]

[What you're doing right now to fix it — specific action, specific timeline]

[What you're doing to prevent recurrence — brief]

If there's anything else I can clarify, or if the resolution above doesn't fully address your concern, please reply and I'll personally ensure it's handled.

[Your name]
[Direct contact / extension]

## What not to do

### Don't use "I'm sorry you feel that way"

This is widely recognized as a non-apology. It implies the problem is how they're feeling, not what happened. It makes things worse.

### Don't over-explain or make excuses

"Our system was under maintenance, and our provider had an outage, and we were short-staffed that day..." The customer doesn't care why it happened — they care that it happened and that you're fixing it.

One sentence of explanation is fine. More than that reads as deflection.

### Don't offer compensation before acknowledging the problem

Jumping to "we'll give you 20% off your next order" before properly acknowledging what went wrong feels like bribery rather than resolution.

### Don't delay

A complaint email that waits 48+ hours for a response is a customer who has already decided to leave. Acknowledge within 4 hours even if you don't have the full answer yet: "I've received your email and I'm looking into it now. I'll have a full response for you by [specific time]."

## The escalation case

Some customers will not be satisfied no matter what. Signs you're dealing with an escalation:
- Multiple complaint emails in a short period
- CC of a supervisor, regulator, or lawyer
- Explicit threat of public review or legal action

In these cases:
1. Move to phone within 24 hours if possible
2. Involve a manager in the response (or let them take over)
3. Document everything in writing
4. Don't make promises you haven't cleared with the relevant department
