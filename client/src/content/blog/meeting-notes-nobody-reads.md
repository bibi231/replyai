The average professional attends 25 meetings per week. Most generate some form of notes. Almost none of those notes are read by anyone who wasn't in the meeting — and often they're not re-read by the people who were.

This isn't a discipline problem. It's a format problem.

## Why traditional meeting notes fail

Traditional meeting notes try to be a transcript. They capture what was said, in the order it was said, from the person who happened to have the keyboard. This format has three problems:

**1. No hierarchy.** Everything has equal weight — a background discussion about a side topic sits next to the decision that determines the next quarter's roadmap.

**2. No action clarity.** "We agreed to look into the vendor options" is not an action item. Who looks into it? By when? What does "look into" mean?

**3. No audience awareness.** The CEO doesn't need the same level of detail as the engineer who has to implement the decision.

## The format that gets read

Meeting notes that get used have three sections and only three:

### Section 1: Summary (3 sentences max)
What did this meeting cover, what was the key decision or outcome, and what's the immediate next step? Anyone who didn't attend can read this and know whether they need to read further.

### Section 2: Decisions
Bullet list of decisions made. Not discussions, not options considered — only actual decisions.

Format: "DECIDED: [what was decided] — [who decided it / by what authority]"

Example:
- DECIDED: Launch date moved to Q3 — approved by product team
- DECIDED: Vendor B selected for payment processing — final selection pending legal review
- DECIDED: Engineering freeze extended 2 weeks — engineering lead

### Section 3: Action items
Every action item has three fields: **what**, **who**, **by when**. No exceptions.

| Action | Owner | Due |
|---|---|---|
| Send revised scope to client | Emeka | Friday 5pm |
| Review contract terms | Legal (Tunde) | Wednesday EOD |
| Update project plan | PM | Monday |

## What to cut

Most meeting notes contain things that belong in other formats:

- **Background context** → belongs in the meeting agenda, not the notes
- **Who said what** → almost never needed; decisions and actions are what matter
- **Options that were considered and rejected** → unless a future team needs to understand why Option A was chosen, cut it

## Distribution and follow-up

Notes sent within 2 hours of a meeting are read. Notes sent 2 days later are mostly archived unread.

Set a rule: notes are sent before the next working hour boundary. If the meeting ends at 3pm, notes are out before 5pm.

Tag action item owners directly in the notes (email, Slack, WhatsApp — wherever your team actually communicates). Don't assume people will re-read a group email to find their name.
