Following up on emails is uncomfortable for most professionals. You don't want to seem desperate, aggressive, or annoying — but you also need a response. This guide shows how to do it well.

## The core principle

A firm follow-up is confident, not apologetic. It doesn't:
- Grovel ("I'm so sorry to bother you again...")
- Guilt-trip ("I've emailed three times with no response...")
- Threaten ("If I don't hear from you by tomorrow...")

It does:
- State clearly what you need
- Set a reasonable deadline
- Leave the door open for a different outcome if circumstances have changed

## The timing

| Wait | Then... |
|---|---|
| 3 business days after initial email | First follow-up |
| 4–5 business days after first follow-up | Second follow-up |
| 7–10 business days after second follow-up | Final follow-up / close the loop |

Don't follow up after 24 hours unless it's explicitly urgent. That's pressure, not persistence.

## Template 1: Simple first follow-up

Subject: Re: [Original subject line]

Hi [Name],

Just following up on the email below — I wanted to make sure it didn't get lost.

[One sentence reminder of the ask or the key point]

Let me know if you need any additional information.

[Your name]

---

## Template 2: Second follow-up (slightly more direct)

Subject: Following up — [specific topic]

Hi [Name],

I'm following up again on [specific topic]. I know things get busy — I just want to make sure this is on your radar.

[One line restatement of what you need and by when]

If this isn't the right priority right now, please let me know and I'll reach out another time.

[Your name]

---

## Template 3: Final follow-up (firm close)

Subject: Final follow-up — [specific topic]

Hi [Name],

I've reached out a couple of times about [topic] and haven't heard back. I don't want to keep filling your inbox, so I'll take this as a signal that the timing isn't right.

If circumstances change and [what you offered / asked] becomes relevant, I'd be happy to reconnect.

[Your name]

---

## Template 4: Overdue payment (escalating firmness)

**First reminder (before due date):**
"A quick reminder that Invoice #[number] is due [date]. Please let me know if you need anything from my side."

**Second reminder (3 days overdue):**
"Invoice #[number] for ₦[amount] was due [date] and doesn't appear to have been settled. Please confirm when to expect payment."

**Third reminder (10+ days overdue):**
"This is a formal notice that Invoice #[number] for ₦[amount], due [date], remains unpaid. Please settle by [new date] or contact me to discuss. If I don't hear from you, I'll need to pause further work and explore other options."

## What "firm" actually sounds like

Compare:

**Too apologetic:** "I'm so sorry to bother you, I know you're super busy, but I was just wondering if maybe you'd had a chance to review my proposal at all? No pressure, just wanted to check in!"

**Firm and professional:** "Following up on the proposal I sent on June 3rd. I'd like to hear your thoughts before end of week — happy to jump on a quick call if that's easier than email."

The second version:
- States the timeline clearly
- Shows confidence that the proposal has value
- Offers a path forward
- No apologies for following up

## One more thing

The best follow-up is one you don't need to send — because your initial email was clear, direct, and made it easy for the person to respond. But when follow-ups are needed, the templates above handle the tone correctly.
